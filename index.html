Kevin's Natural Foods
kevinsnaturalfoods.com - CPG / Sauces - Health-Conscious Consumer - Multi-Channel
52% YoY Growth - #1 in Sauces - 3.9x ROAS
PPC What Ridgeline Did Right The Playbook

Ridgeline built a four-phase flywheel: win a tight subcategory first, expand into adjacent terms, time spend around the Q1 health spike, and optimize PDPs to hold the rank gains. The 57% organic / 43% paid sales split is the proof it worked - that ratio shows real brand equity, not just ad dependency.

  • Phase 1 subcategory domination was the right call - starting with "simmer sauce," "keto sauce," and "paleo sauce" built algorithmic relevance before competing on broader terms where CPCs are higher and competition is fiercer.
  • Health-forward Q1 timing is smart seasonal strategy - aligning January spend ramp with the wellness resolution spike is exactly how you acquire new-to-brand customers at the lowest ACoS of the year, then let organic momentum carry the rest.
  • The 26% ACoS while scaling is the real win - maintaining efficiency while growing spend is the hardest thing to do on Amazon. Most brands see ACoS inflate as they scale. Holding 26% means the keyword targeting stayed disciplined.
SEO What I Would Do Next as the Analyst My Contribution
  • Build a flavor-level keyword matrix - each SKU has its own unique keyword universe ("tikka masala sauce," "thai peanut sauce," "lemon herb simmer sauce"). Separate campaigns per flavor with flavor-specific backend terms unlocks longtail ranking that a brand-level campaign misses.
  • Competitor conquesting audit on a monthly cadence - the top 10 competitors in each sauce subcategory change. I would run a monthly search term report cross-referenced against competitor ASINs to identify which competitor placements are converting and adjust Sponsored Display bids accordingly.
  • Automate the Q1 spend ramp - the seasonal flywheel is proven. I would build an Excel model that projects the exact weekly budget increases from January through March based on prior year velocity data, so the ramp is pre-approved and ready to execute without scrambling for budget approval in December.
  • A/B test health claims in bullet points - "keto-friendly" vs. "keto certified" vs. "fits your keto lifestyle" - small copy changes in bullet point 1 can meaningfully shift CVR for health-motivated shoppers. Set up the test in Manage Your Experiments and run it for 8 weeks.
Salomon
salomon.com - Footwear / Outdoor - Global Brand - New-to-Brand Acquisition
$1.8M Ad Sales - 65% New-to-Brand - Top 20 BSR Every Shoe Category
PPC What Ridgeline Did Right The Playbook

Salomon was 100% branded search - the advertising equivalent of only talking to people who already know you. Ridgeline flipped it to 80% broad category terms, used all three ad types in a unified strategy, and drove 65% new-to-brand sales. That is a textbook acquisition overhaul.

  • Shifting from 100% branded to 80% category terms was the highest-leverage move - branded search has a ceiling. Category and competitor terms are where new customers live. The willingness to reduce branded efficiency in the short term to build NTB volume long term takes strategic conviction most brands avoid.
  • Sponsored Display on competitor PDPs was smart targeting - appearing below the buy box of competing trail running shoes is the highest-intent placement available. That 25% of sales coming from a rival product page proves the shopper was already in buying mode.
  • Using Sponsored Brands to introduce the product line by category - trail running, backpacking, hiking as separate ad sets - lets the creative do category-specific work rather than generic brand messaging.
SEO What I Would Do Next as the Analyst My Contribution
  • Build a negative keyword architecture for each category - trail running, backpacking, and casual hiking have very different buyer intent. Without tight negatives, Salomon trail shoes appear on searches for dress shoes or sandals and burn budget on zero-intent clicks. I would pull the search term report weekly and build category-level negative lists.
  • Segment NTB campaigns from retention campaigns in reporting - 65% NTB is impressive but the follow-up question is: are those customers coming back? I would build a report tracking repeat purchase rate by keyword source to determine which acquisition terms produce loyal customers vs. one-time buyers.
  • Leverage Amazon Attribution for off-Amazon traffic tagging - Salomon has massive social and influencer presence. Attribution links on every influencer post and social bio let you see exactly how much Amazon revenue social drives, which justifies the off-Amazon spend and gives you data to optimize the influencer mix.
  • Run seasonal bid adjustments by category - trail running peaks in spring, backpacking in summer, winter footwear in fall. Pre-built bid multipliers by season reduce manual work and keep each category competitive when its audience is most active.
Grundens
grundens.com - Commercial Fishing Gear / Outerwear - 2PxP Seller Model - Margin Focus
+23% Gross Sales - +15 Margin Points - 13% Storefront CVR
OPS What Ridgeline Did Right The Playbook

The 2PxP model is brilliant for brands that want Amazon control without the operational overhead of running their own Seller Central account. Ridgeline took full ownership of catalog, inventory, ads, and reporting, and in doing so unlocked margins that exceeded Grundens' own wholesale margins. That is a hard thing to pull off.

  • Right-to-win category focus on footwear was correct - spending 50% of ad budget on competitor conquesting in a category where Grundens boots were technically superior at comparable price is exactly the right call. You go where you can win, not where you have history.
  • The storefront achieving 13% CVR - four times the DTC industry average - is exceptional - this comes from building the storefront as a branded experience that mirrors the DTC site rather than a generic Amazon catalog dump.
  • FBA for fast movers, FBM for slower items via 3PL - this split approach to fulfillment keeps storage fees controlled while maintaining Prime eligibility on the high-velocity SKUs that drive most of the revenue.
SEO What I Would Do Next as the Analyst My Contribution
  • Build a category expansion roadmap beyond footwear - once footwear hit 3:1 ROAS and proved its right to win, the next step is identifying the second category with the strongest product differentiation vs. Amazon best sellers. I would run a gap analysis: pull the top 20 competitors in each outerwear subcategory, compare specs and price point, and rank Grundens' SKUs by competitive advantage.
  • Backend keyword refresh on a quarterly cadence - commercial fishing, recreational fishing, and sailing have overlapping but distinct keyword universes. Backend search terms go stale as search behavior shifts. A quarterly review using Helium 10 Cerebro on top competitor ASINs keeps the catalog indexed for current search patterns.
  • A+ Content upgrade on the top 20% of SKUs by revenue - the image carousel optimization worked for conversion. The next layer is A+ Content modules that reinforce the "why buy" question with material technology callouts, weather rating charts, and use-case imagery that separates Grundens from private label competitors on visual quality alone.
  • Build a cross-sell storefront flow by use case - "commercial fishing outfit," "deck hand kit," "offshore wet weather kit" - curated collections within the storefront that bundle jackets, bibs, and boots increase basket size and storefront session value beyond the 13% CVR baseline.
Badlands
badlandsgear.com - Premium Hunting Gear - Storefront Strategy - NTB Acquisition
7.5% Storefront CVR - 46% Storefront Sales from SB Ads - 41% Organic Discovery
SB What Ridgeline Did Right The Playbook

Badlands is a premium brand in a category where Amazon buyers are skeptical of quality. Building a storefront that matches the off-Amazon brand experience - rather than looking like a generic Amazon listing - is what produces a 7.5% CVR. The 41% organic discovery rate means the storefront is also indexed and ranking in Google, which is a long-term SEO asset most brands completely ignore.

  • Using Sponsored Brands specifically to drive storefront traffic was the right architecture - SB ads pointing to the storefront introduce the full product line rather than a single ASIN, which increases basket size and brand familiarity simultaneously.
  • 72% NTB orders from the Sponsored Brand campaign is the headline number - that means 7 out of 10 storefront visitors had never bought Badlands before. The storefront converted them. That is brand-building at the point of purchase.
  • Designing for the brand experience first, not Amazon defaults - most brands build storefronts that look like Amazon. Badlands' storefront looks like Badlands. That brand consistency is what drives the 61% higher sales per visit from organic storefront visitors vs. other traffic sources.
SEO What I Would Do Next as the Analyst My Contribution
  • Build Amazon Attribution links for every off-Amazon channel - the storefront is already indexed in Google and converting organic traffic at a high rate. Adding Attribution tags to Badlands' Instagram, YouTube, email campaigns, and any influencer partnerships lets you measure exactly how much revenue each external channel drives to Amazon and optimize accordingly.
  • Expand the storefront into a seasonal content hub - elk season, mule deer season, archery opener - hunting has a tight, passionate, well-defined seasonal calendar. A storefront that rotates featured collections and seasonal imagery by hunt type keeps the brand relevant for repeat visits and gives Sponsored Brand campaigns fresh creative to rotate through.
  • Layer Sponsored Display retargeting on top of the SB acquisition funnel - a shopper who visited the Badlands storefront but did not purchase is a warm audience. Sponsored Display retargeting keeps Badlands in front of that shopper for the next 30 days, closing the purchase window that SB opened.
  • Track storefront CVR by traffic source in weekly reporting - the 7.5% blended CVR is the starting benchmark. Breaking it down by SB traffic, organic discovery, and PDP click-through shows which source produces the highest-intent visitors, which informs where to allocate future ad spend to maximize storefront ROI.
My Amazon Philosophy

How I Think About
Advertising That Sticks.

Ridgeline's philosophy and mine are aligned. Here's how I approach the work.

01

TACoS Over ROAS

ROAS alone is a vanity metric on Amazon. Total Advertising Cost of Sales tells you whether your ad spend is growing the business or just recycling existing demand. I always report both and explain the difference to stakeholders.

02

Ads Serve Organic Rank

Every Sponsored Products campaign I build has an organic ranking thesis. Sales velocity from the right keywords feeds the algorithm. The goal is to eventually reduce ad dependency, not increase it indefinitely.

03

Copy and Keywords Together

Listing copy and keyword strategy can't be separate workflows. The title, bullets, and backend terms need to be built from the same keyword research. I handle both so nothing falls through the gap between "the SEO person" and "the copy person."

04

Negative Keywords Are Half the Job

Wasted spend on irrelevant searches is the fastest way to inflate ACoS. My AI system at JLS was built specifically to catch this at scale. Clean search term reports, aggressive negatives, and a weekly review cadence keep budgets working efficiently.

05

Brand First, Platform Second

Ridgeline's core belief - "we conform the platform to your brand" - is exactly right. Amazon's tools are levers. The brand's positioning, voice, and differentiators are what you build the strategy around. I read brand guidelines before touching a campaign.

06

Reports That Drive Action

A report that just shows numbers isn't useful. Every performance update I produce includes what changed, why it changed, what I'm doing about it, and what we're watching next week. That's what cross-functional teams actually need.
